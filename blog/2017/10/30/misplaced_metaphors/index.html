Pre-script: I was fortunate enough to have been introduced to the age-old collection of Indian fables that I still hold dear: Stories from the Pañcatantra and Jataka Tales… The exciting and wonderful anthropomorphic world portrayed was my first foray into the moral dimensions and quite possibly the makings of me.

A dear friend of mine has been fixated on a book he came across Natural Enmity: Reflections on the Niti and Rasa of the Pancatantra

I recently got lobbed a juicy full toss with a terribly misplaced metaphor, demonstrating how reasoning-by-analogy can be tenuous. Making a priori assumptions about human nature (ie: arriving at conclusions from theoretical inferences rather than empirical evidence) from sagacious political guides from a bygone era may not be very prescriptive today: as the parameters with which we are playing the game of life has changed dramatically!

As I was penning down my thoughts after reading some stories from Panchathantra for my research, I realized where we are talking past each other:

To my dear friend,

What you are calling moral (in terms of Natural Enmity, which in retrospect is apparent from your Lion-Deer example) is what I call a survival strategy.. However, the objective morals I am talking about is the set of rules that that all the Lions inevitably follow, that results in a stable solution the Lotka-Volterra model for Predetor-Prey dynamics. The inevitability is the ‘Natural’ aspect of these sort of mathematical models that is working on the premise that the agents in the model have no perceivable meta-awareness of their situation and act without free-will (which is a Whole Other Conversation!.. as David Hume rightly called it ‘the most contentious question of metaphysics!’)

My point is that it is not necessary to submit to all-things-natural just because it is only natural
A conscious entity, who is willful enough to cause change and strong enough to act on it can subvert the stability of any mathematical model.

The Answer is Choice!

Sometimes it becomes a game to point out where an analogy breaks down, instead of focussing on the parallel as just a device to illustrate a comparison to think about a problem. Of course not all analogies are evil, but certain comparisons are at best wrongheaded and at worst, deliberately malicious: intending to use rhetoric just to win the argument!

To dissect your example:

a. in the abstract:

Though the dharma of the Lion is different from that of the Deer, you can see that the morality for Lion doesn’t change with Lion of a different Forest.

Predators be predators

There are things that are different in character in the above example. While it is always endearing to our inner-child to see anthropomorphic characters engaged in a battle of wits, applying the morals preached in fables as general guidelines to base societies off of may be a sub-optimal strategy.

b. and in the real world:

If you are going to draw comparisons with the food chain, understand that morality is a concept that is supposed to fight the natural order of animalistic actions..

The world today is no more a zero-sum game:
now, it is not necessary for someone to lose for another to win!

Forming a society to sustainably encourage spreading of comfort, not just to the immediate progeny, is the central tenet of our civilization project.

Struggle for Existance

Philosophy is like Rain Man:
a slow elder brother, who lives in the dark, can’t help you out directly,
but without whom you wouldn’t be here today.

so what to do?

The Moral Landscape (Sam Harris) has convinced me that Absolute morality, founded on objective claims, is the same for a species as a collective!
I am not saying that different traditions need the have the same value system, but the moral foundation would need to align. Afterall, they seem to share all other valuable additions that advances in Science and Technology has to offer like in medicine, automation, trade, etc: all arising from a proper understanding of Physics!

Need for co-operation

Tit for tat may lose battles, but wins wars!!

If we base our beliefs on incorrect foundational ideas, it is not surprising that we end up making bad decisions…
With a fundamental premise that reality is unique and is not subject quirks in our sense perceptions (like the Nyaya school of thought which observational inferences professes), the conclusion to draw from that is to see if the net-sum wellbeing of all individuals can be maximized through certain actions, isn’t it a good idea to do more of those actions?

Feynman’s approach to tackling philosophy

It is of course not a simple question to answer, but you should watch out for r/badphilosophy. Here’s a good litmus test to see if you should consider any philosophical outlook that someone is promulgating:

  1. Hear out their point-of-view
  2. Take the exact opposite of it and see how the world would be different if it were true..
  3. if nothing is going to be different, then it might as well be either!
